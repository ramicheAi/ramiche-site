The Timeline

Every breakthrough that built the industry, layer by layer.

SLA
1984
Chuck Hull Invents Stereolithography
Working at a small tabletop coatings company, Charles "Chuck" Hull filed the first patent for stereolithography — using UV light to cure photopolymer resin layer by layer. Nobody understood it. He built the first SLA-1 machine in his garage. The age of additive manufacturing began with a beam of ultraviolet light.
Resolution ~150μm
Build Time Days
Cost $300K+
SLS
1988
Carl Deckard Patents Selective Laser Sintering
At UT Austin, Carl Deckard and Joe Beaman developed SLS — a laser fusing powder particles together. No support structures needed. Nylon, metal, ceramic — anything that could be powdered could be printed. This was the technology that would eventually produce functional end-use parts, not just prototypes.
Material Nylon Powder
Advantage No Supports
Cost $400K+
FDM
1989
Scott Crump Invents FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling)
Scott Crump, co-founder of Stratasys, invented FDM by accident while trying to make a toy frog for his daughter using a hot glue gun and a mixture of polyethylene and candle wax. He realized he could automate the process — extruding thermoplastic filament through a heated nozzle, building objects layer by layer. This would become the most widely-used 3D printing technology on Earth.
Material ABS Filament
Nozzle Temp ~260°C
Impact Foundation of Desktop 3D Printing
REP
2005
RepRap Project — The Self-Replicating Printer
Dr. Adrian Bowyer at the University of Bath launched RepRap — an open-source FDM printer that could print most of its own parts. This was the philosophical earthquake. If a machine could reproduce itself, the economics of manufacturing would invert. The open-source community exploded. MakerBot, Prusa, and an entire generation of desktop printers trace their DNA to RepRap.
Cost ~$500
Self-Replication ~50% of parts
Legacy MakerBot, Prusa, Ender
FFF
2009
FDM Patent Expires — The Democratization
Stratasys' FDM patent expired. Overnight, anyone could build and sell FDM printers. MakerBot launched the Cupcake CNC. Kickstarter campaigns flooded in. The price of a 3D printer dropped from $100,000+ to under $1,000. The maker movement had its machine.
Price Drop 100x
New Companies 200+
MakerBot $1,299
DLP
2012
Formlabs Form 1 — Desktop Resin Printing
Formlabs' Kickstarter raised $2.9M — the highest-funded 3D printer campaign at the time. The Form 1 brought SLA-quality resin printing to the desktop for $3,299. Suddenly jewelers, dentists, and engineers could produce parts with 25μm resolution without a six-figure machine. The resin revolution began.
Resolution 25μm
Price $3,299
Kickstarter $2.9M raised
CLIP
2014
Carbon CLIP — Continuous Liquid Interface Production
Carbon's CLIP technology eliminated the peel step in resin printing by projecting UV through an oxygen-permeable window, creating a "dead zone" of uncured resin that allows continuous printing. 25-100x faster than traditional SLA. Adidas used it to mass-produce 4D midsoles. 3D printing crossed from prototyping into mass production.
Speed 25-100x faster
Customer Adidas 4D
Valuation $2.4B
MJF
2016
HP Multi Jet Fusion — Industrial Speed at Scale
HP entered 3D printing with MJF — using inkjet heads to selectively apply fusing and detailing agents to a powder bed, then flash-fusing with infrared energy. 10x faster than SLS, with better surface finish and functional part density. HP brought its inkjet manufacturing scale to 3D. Production volumes became viable.
Speed 10x vs SLS
Voxel Control Yes
Machine Cost $130K+
COVID
2020
COVID-19 — 3D Printing Proves Its Value
When global supply chains collapsed, 3D printers stepped in. Makers printed face shields, ventilator adapters, nasopharyngeal swabs, and PPE clips. Hospitals ran print farms 24/7. Prusa delivered 200,000+ face shields. The world saw the strategic value of distributed manufacturing. Governments took notice.
Face Shields Millions printed
Prusa Alone 200K+ shields
Industry Growth +21% in 2020
BBL
2022
Bambu Lab — The Apple of 3D Printing
A team of ex-DJI engineers launched Bambu Lab and redefined what a consumer 3D printer could be. The X1 Carbon shipped with auto bed leveling, LiDAR first-layer detection, an AMS (Automatic Material System) for multi-color printing, and CoreXY speeds up to 500mm/s. It "just worked." The community went from tolerating 3D printers to loving them.
Speed 500mm/s
Auto-Level LiDAR
Multi-Color AMS (4 spools)
AI
2024-2026
AI + 3D Printing Convergence
Text-to-3D models via Meshy, Rodin, Tripo. AI-generated slicer profiles. Computer vision failure detection mid-print. Generative design tools producing organic structures no human would design. The printer is becoming an output device for AI creativity. The barrier to entry dropped from "learn CAD" to "describe what you want."
Text-to-3D <60 seconds
AI Slicing Adaptive profiles
Failure Detection Real-time CV

Technology Deep Dive

How each printing technology actually works — with animated layer demos.

SLA — Stereolithography

UV laser cures liquid photopolymer resin
A UV laser traces each layer's cross-section on the surface of a vat of liquid resin. The resin cures (hardens) where the laser hits. The build platform lifts, a new layer of liquid flows in, and the process repeats. Incredible detail, smooth surfaces, but requires post-curing and support removal.
Resolution
25μm
Speed
Slow
Strength
Medium
Cost/Part
$$

FDM — Fused Deposition Modeling

Heated nozzle extrudes thermoplastic filament
A spool of thermoplastic filament feeds into a heated nozzle (190-300°C). The molten material is extruded in thin lines, building each layer like a very precise hot glue gun. The bed or nozzle moves in XYZ. Visible layer lines, but incredibly versatile — PLA, ABS, PETG, TPU, carbon fiber, wood fill, metal fill.
Resolution
100μm
Speed
Fast
Strength
Good
Cost/Part
$

SLS — Selective Laser Sintering

Laser fuses powder particles in a heated chamber
A thin layer of nylon powder is spread across the build area. A CO2 laser selectively fuses the powder at each cross-section. The bed drops, new powder is spread, repeat. The unfused powder acts as its own support — meaning complex geometries, interlocking parts, and internal channels print without any support structures. Industrial workhorse.
Resolution
80μm
Speed
Medium
Strength
High
Cost/Part
$$

DLP — Digital Light Processing

Projector cures entire resin layer at once
Instead of a single laser tracing each point, DLP uses a digital projector to flash an entire layer's image onto the resin simultaneously. Every pixel cures at once — making it dramatically faster than SLA for dense prints. The trade-off: resolution is limited by pixel size. Dominant in dental, jewelry, and miniatures.
Resolution
35μm
Speed
Fast
Strength
Medium
Cost/Part
$$

MJF — Multi Jet Fusion

Inkjet fusing agents + infrared energy on powder bed
HP's approach: spread nylon powder, then inkjet heads deposit fusing agent (absorbs IR) and detailing agent (inhibits fusion at edges) with voxel-level precision. An infrared lamp passes over — fusing agent areas melt, detailing areas stay crisp. 10x faster than SLS with better surface finish. The production printing breakthrough.
Resolution
70μm
Speed
Very Fast
Strength
High
Cost/Part
$

Bioprinting

Living cells deposited in bioink scaffolds
Bioprinters extrude "bioinks" — hydrogels containing living cells — to build tissue structures layer by layer. The scaffold provides shape while cells grow, connect, and form functional tissue. Skin grafts, cartilage, bone scaffolds, and organoids are already in clinical trials. Full organ printing is the horizon goal. This is where 3D printing stops being manufacturing and becomes medicine.

The Future

Where additive manufacturing is headed — and what it means for everything.

2027
AI-Native Slicer Engines
Slicer software will use transformer models trained on millions of prints to auto-generate optimal profiles. Upload a model, the AI reads the geometry, material, and your printer's calibration data, and produces settings that outperform any human tuning. Manual slicer profiles become legacy.
Likelihood:
90%
2028
Sub-$100 Multi-Material Printers
The Bambu effect cascades. Competition from Creality, Anycubic, and Chinese OEMs drives a capable multi-color, auto-leveling, enclosed FDM printer below $100. 3D printing becomes as common as an inkjet. Schools, libraries, and households adopt en masse.
Likelihood:
80%
2029
Bioprinted Skin in Hospitals
Burn units will use bedside bioprinters to deposit autologous skin grafts — printed from the patient's own cells, directly onto wound sites. No donor site, no rejection, faster healing. The first FDA-approved bioprinted tissue product ships. The organ waitlist starts to shrink.
Likelihood:
65%
2030
Lunar Regolith Printing
NASA's Artemis program deploys the first autonomous construction printer on the Moon, using sintered lunar regolith (moon dust) to build radiation-shielded habitats. No material shipped from Earth. The same technology scales to Mars. 3D printing becomes the first construction method used on another world.
Likelihood:
50%
2032
Self-Healing Printed Structures
Materials embedded with microcapsules of healing agents allow printed parts to repair their own cracks. A fracture ruptures the capsule, releasing monomer that polymerizes and seals the damage. Structural prints in bridges, buildings, and aircraft get lifespans measured in centuries, not decades.
Likelihood:
55%
2035
Molecular-Scale Printing
Two-photon polymerization advances to nanometer resolution, enabling printed metamaterials with programmable optical, acoustic, and mechanical properties. A printed lens that bends light in ways glass cannot. A printed bone scaffold that's stronger than bone. Manufacturing at the molecular scale changes what "material" means.
Likelihood:
40%
